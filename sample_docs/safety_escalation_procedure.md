# Safety Escalation Procedure

**Document ID:** OPS-SAFE-001
**Version:** 1.0
**Department:** Safety and Operations
**Document Type:** Safety Procedure
**Effective Date:** 2026-01-01
**Classification:** Synthetic demo document

## 1. Purpose

This procedure defines how safety incidents, unsafe conditions, near misses and operational risks must be escalated. The purpose is to ensure critical risks are communicated quickly, decisions are documented and personnel remain protected.

## 2. Incident Severity Levels

Level 1 is a minor condition with low risk, such as a minor housekeeping issue or a missing non-critical label. It must be recorded before the end of the shift.

Level 2 is a moderate condition requiring supervisor attention, such as damaged guarding without exposed rotating parts, repeated minor alarms or a localized slip hazard. It must be reported to the shift supervisor within two hours.

Level 3 is a serious condition that may cause injury, equipment damage, environmental impact or operational disruption. Examples include exposed energized components, leakage near electrical equipment, abnormal vibration on rotating equipment, loss of safety interlock or repeated overheating alarm. Level 3 must be escalated immediately to the operations manager and safety coordinator.

Level 4 is a critical condition representing immediate danger to personnel, major equipment, environment or facility continuity. Examples include fire, smoke, serious injury, explosion risk, uncontrolled chemical release, arc flash or structural instability. Level 4 requires immediate stop-work action and emergency response activation.

## 3. Immediate Stop-Work Criteria

Any employee or contractor has authority to stop work when an unsafe condition is observed. Work must be stopped immediately when personnel are exposed to energized equipment without protection, confined space entry lacks authorization, lifting becomes unstable, fire or explosion risk is detected, a critical safety barrier is missing, a worker is injured, severe weather creates unsafe conditions or isolation status is uncertain.

After stop-work, personnel must move to a safe location, notify the shift supervisor, secure the area if safe, wait for formal authorization before resuming and record the event.

## 4. Escalation Matrix

Level 1 must be notified to the area technician or responsible owner before the end of the shift. Level 2 must be notified to the shift supervisor within two hours. Level 3 must be notified immediately to the operations manager and safety coordinator. Level 4 must be notified immediately to the emergency response team, operations manager and site director.

For Level 3 and Level 4 events, communication must be verbal first and documented afterward. If the shift supervisor cannot be reached, the technician must contact the operations control center directly.

## 5. Communication Requirements

All safety escalation messages must include location, date and time, personnel involved, description of the unsafe condition, immediate actions taken, current area status, whether work has been stopped, whether emergency services are required and available evidence.

Communication must be clear and factual. The person reporting must avoid assumptions about root cause until the investigation is complete. For critical events, updates must be provided every 30 minutes until the situation is stabilized.

## 6. Evidence Collection

Evidence must be collected when safe. Evidence may include photographs, alarm logs, control system screenshots, witness statements, permits, inspection records, equipment trends, maintenance history and environmental readings.

Evidence must not be modified, deleted or overwritten. If the area is unsafe, collection must wait until the safety coordinator or emergency response team releases the area.
